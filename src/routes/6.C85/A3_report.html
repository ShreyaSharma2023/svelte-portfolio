Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The data shows a slowly increasing trend from mid March 2020 onwards and three more distinct peaks (and surrounding troughs) seen in Nov, Dec and Jan 2021 before declining to a minima in June, another maxima in Sep and decreasing with small peaks before an almost exponential rise in Jan 2022.
  • At first glance it seems like the data for the two years 2020 and 2021 almost has a 'lag'. Jun 2020 has a local minima whereas the minima in 2021 is coser to July.
  • It seems like Aug-Nov 2021 was the Delta variant peak and Dec 2022 onwards was the Omicron variant peak. I also tried to see if there is a correlation in the data and the swearing in of the new government in the US, in Jan 2021.

Design Critique:

  • I was curious about the need of a symmetrical visualisation about the two sides of the inner spiral radius, since the data did not encode any variable which could be shown on the two sides. The spiral helped in giving an idea about the continuous nature of the data and helped give insights about seasonal trends, but it also gave a false perception of depth due to the central focus point.
  • The visualisation shows only the moving average of the new confirmed cases, but did not show the uncertainty or the projections the article talks about. The commentary about the strain on hospitals due to varying severity of the different strains was not captured here. The important data was not intentionally hidden, but the reader would subconsciously compare the number of cases to the radius of the circle, since they do not align on the same axis. As this is published in an online newspaper, people from all backgrounds are the target audience, and the visualisation is simple enough for everyone to understand when the next wave may peak.
  • I think the takeaway message is still open to interpretation a bit, the visualisation shows when and how much the peak is expected to happen, but the article also has a discussion about effects of a vaccine, comparison to other countries in similar and different seasons and the deadliness of the variants along with hospital strains, so the reader may assume a need to take unnecessary caution since only the case numbers are presented. I wish the names of all the months were written for completeness, and the 7 day average label could have been mentioned elsewhere. The spacing feels deceptive, since the mind subconsciously compares the white space due to thickness of the visualisation. Arrows make it clear where to begin reading.

Visualization Sketches

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • I wanted the visualisation to be able to compare the number of cases (14 day sum) on an aligned scale (same radius), so that the seasonal/monthly trends could be compared more easily.
  • The peaks and troughs stand out more distinctly on first glance since they are on an aligned scale. However, the centre of the circle is too crowded and the scale does not work well, since there is a lot of unused white space. I suspect this is what may have prompted the author to use a spiral template.
  • I want to explore putting this data on a traditional rectangle graph, so that the reader does not have to move their eyes around to comprehend it, and there is no deceptive use of the radius of the circle.

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Here I compared the three years (14 day sum) as bar graphs, which gives the reader an idea of the trends between the months in each year, without having to put the extra strain of going around the circle.
  • The thickness of the bar graph gives an illusion of area, which makes it easier to grasp. However, the colors do not look distinct enough, and there is still crowding near the lower values of the y axis.
  • The seasonal trend seems to play a factor in the number of cases, hence I would like to move away from using color, and focus on grouping cases by season and compare them for different years which would help people have a better idea of long term trends.

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • I used a human as a mark this time, to keep in mind the human issue at hand. The objective here was to group the trends by seasons in the Northern Hemisphere, and quickly give some insight into the scale of the problem without going into details of the fine small scale temporal trends
  • There was visually lesser data on the chart, which was not as overwhelming for the reader, and the main message of the scale of the trends was conveyed. I could have used more color for the different seasons and given more hints as to how to read the chart
  • To improve, I can try drawing the marks in the same pattern, i.e. x number of symbols on a line, since I sometimes found myself countign the number of symbols, which could have been made more clear.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

Through the final visualisation, I am trying to give a more holistic picture of the covid crisis by comparing several differnt metrics. I want to compare the three years, since the seasons have an effect on people's activities as well as the natural cycel of infection, hence they are colored differently. I also want people to be aware of data about how much testing is being done (since the reading group article brought up some good points) and what fraction of that have confirmed cases, and also the fact that each confirmed case has two outcomes - either recovery or death. I want people to look at this graph and make conclusions abotut the differnt peaks, and vaccine efficacy (as shown by decreasing fraction of death over the years). I used the area chart for this since it is the easiest to view and contextualise in terms of yearly data. Making several wide graphs was suitable due to the shape of the data arranged in different months. I used darker hues for more recent years and used the same color scheme across years for consistency. All of them were on a linear scale except for confirmed fraction which was logarithmic due to the raneg of the data. As for the computed fractions, confirmed was [new confirmed] / [new tested] and recovered and deceased were [new recovered/deceased] / [new confirmed] respectively. These transformations make an effetive argument since the reader can logically follow the outcome of a case. The visualisation does hide data about testing in the later dates, specifically since testing in 2022 was very low, and coupled with data issues in the [new recovered] column made the [recovered fraction] not as reliable as the others.

Reflection

The visualisation addressed most of my critiques of the original graph, like giving the reader an easy way to compare yearly data across seasons, not being deceptive about different scales, and providing more context about the problem at hand, with the reader having the chance to look at trends and recovery rates, evaluate the efficacy of vaccines, look at the testing rates, etc. The three years can bee seen distinctly in different colors, and hence their reltive differences stand out clearly. However, it is far from perfect. There were inconstencies in the logical flow I wanted to see clearly of testing -> Confirmed -> recovery or death due to the data collection method and there being some negative values present which I had to exclude. The sheer amount of data present challenges in communicating this clearly, especially when there are directives we want to give in terms of social distancing, masks etc. I had to make tradeoffs about the scale of the problem, and the ease of comprehension of the problem, which is a critique I had of the original design which I wasn't able to resolve.
